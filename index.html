How it works: three parts

One ticket, one day, three distinct experiences — logistics handled from the moment you board in Calgary.

Part 1

We drive. You relax.

Parkbus handles the two-hour drive from Calgary in a comfortable, air-conditioned coach. Big windows, plenty of storage, and the prairies slowly turning into badlands. We get you there and back — no highway, no navigating, no parking.

Part 2

The Explorers Bus Tour

Join a park-guided tour led by Parks Alberta interpreters deep into the Natural Preserve — the 80% of the park most people never see. Four off-bus stops to view real fossils eroding from the ground, touch 75-million-year-old bone, and maybe lay a protective casting just like field paleontologists do.

Part 3

The park is yours

After the tour, explore at your own pace. Hike the Badlands Interpretive Trail (1.3 km) and the Trail of the Fossil Hunters (0.9 km), climb the hoodoos, scramble the coulees, visit the Visitor Centre, and grab a cold drink at the Cretaceous Café. Several hours of free time.

Walk where dinosaurs roamed

Over 75 million years ago this region was an inland sea. Dinosaurs died in river channels and were buried in layers of sand and mud. Those layers have since eroded into some of the most extraordinary badlands in North America — towering hoodoos, sculpted coulees, terrain that feels genuinely Martian.

More than 300 complete skeletons have been pulled from this valley since the 1880s. You'll walk where paleontologists still uncover bones from the Late Cretaceous, and stand where a Centrosaurus or Hadrosaur once moved.

The heat is real — it's one of Alberta's warmest, driest regions in midsummer — but that's part of the deal. The badlands look even more dramatic in harsh light.

Real bones. Restricted ground.

This isn't a museum behind glass. On the guided tour you'll board a bus into restricted areas you can only reach with a Parks Alberta interpreter, stopping four times to get out and explore fossils still emerging from the rock.

You'll hear stories about the Blackfoot nation — who called the hoodoos matapiiski, "the people" — early fossil hunters from the 1880s, and the modern paleontologists who made discoveries in this exact landscape. Part history, part geology, part storytelling.

It's roughly two hours, designed for all ages and abilities. You're not just sightseeing. You're part of a small expedition.

FAQ: before you go

Everything you need to know about Alberta's strangest, most ancient day trip.

Trip basics & logistics

What's the pickup point and timing?
We depart Calgary early morning (exact time confirmed with your booking — 8:00 AM from Anderson Station Way SW, 7:45 AM from the Stampede Trailblazer Centre). The drive takes about two hours, and we return to Calgary late afternoon. It's a full day, but you're sitting down the whole drive, so pace yourself on the hikes.
Is this a day trip only, or can I stay overnight?
This Parkbus trip is a day trip — you'll return to Calgary the same day. The park does have a campground if you'd like to extend your stay separately.
How far is the park from Calgary?
About 220 kilometres east, near Brooks. Roughly two hours of drive time. That distance is exactly why Parkbus exists — we remove the hassle of navigating the highway yourself.
What's included in my ticket?
Round-trip transportation from Calgary on our coach and the Explorers Bus Tour into the protected Palaeontological Preserve. You get a guided experience with access to restricted fossil areas and off-bus stops to view formations and fossils up close. Your guide is a Parks Alberta interpreter who knows the geology, paleontology, and human history of the site.
What's not included?
Meals, snacks, and additional activities beyond the bus tour (hiking trails are free). Visitor Centre admission and the café are separate. Some guided hikes beyond the bus tour can be booked on-site separately.

The Explorers Bus Tour itself

How long is the guided bus tour?
Approximately two hours. You'll be on and off the bus about four times to view significant fossils, formations, and landscapes.
Will I actually see real fossils, or is this just scenery?
Real fossils. The guided tour takes you to areas where fossils are still eroding out of the ground. You'll have the chance to see — and touch — real dinosaur bones discovered in the field, and you might place a protective casting on a fossil exactly like paleontologists do. To protect the sites, digging isn't permitted, but standing where a Centrosaurus or Hadrosaur once roamed is unforgettable.
What will the guide tell us?
Stories about the people who explored this landscape: the Blackfoot nation (who called the hoodoos matapiiski, "the people"), early fossil hunters from the 1880s onward, and modern paleontologists. You'll learn how the badlands formed, why this area has so many fossils, and what species you're looking at. It's part history, part geology, part paleontology, part storytelling.
Is the bus tour accessible for people with mobility challenges?
Unfortunately the guided bus tour isn't suitable for wheelchairs or strollers due to the terrain. However, the Visitor Centre and some paved areas of the park are accessible.
Can kids come on the bus tour?
Absolutely. Children of all ages are welcome (infants included), though kids under 14 must be accompanied by a paying adult. Fair warning: your dinosaur-obsessed child may never want to leave.
Do I need prior experience or a special fitness level?
Not at all. The Explorers Bus Tour is designed for all ages and abilities — you travel by comfortable bus into the preserve with short stops to view fossils and scenery. If you'd prefer more adventure, the park offers hiking options you can explore on your own during free time.
Will I get bored if I'm not into dinosaurs specifically?
You might be surprised. Even people who think they don't care about paleontology get absorbed. The landscape itself is spectacular — the hoodoos and erosion patterns are geological art — and the human stories add real depth. There's something primal about standing in a place where you can see 75 million years of time on display.

After the tour: hiking & free time

What can I do after the bus tour?
Plenty. The park has five self-guided hiking trails, outdoor fossil displays along the scenic loop road, and a Visitor Centre with interactive exhibits and a theatre. Hike the Badlands Interpretive Trail (1.3 km, easy), follow the Trail of the Fossil Hunters (0.9 km) and keep your eyes down for bone fragments, climb the hoodoos, and scramble the coulees.
How long will I want to stay after the bus tour?
Three to four hours minimum if you're hiking. Less if you just want a coffee and the Badlands Trail; more if you're climbing hoodoos or doing multiple trails. We coordinate a return time that gives everyone free time but gets you back to Calgary at a reasonable hour.
Are there other guided experiences I can add?
Yes. The park offers additional guided hikes (Great Badlands Hike, Centrosaurus Bonebed Hike, and others) that go deeper into the preserve, bookable separately on-site. There's also a Fossil Safari program and hands-on fossil casting classes. These book up quickly in summer, so check availability when you arrive.
Can I bring my own hiking gear?
You don't need specialized gear, but closed-toe, sturdy walking shoes are required (no sandals, flip-flops, or Crocs). Hiking boots or solid running shoes are preferred — participants without appropriate footwear will not be able to attend the program. Wear light clothing, bring a hat and sunglasses, and pack a layer for the air-conditioned ride back.

What to pack & prepare

What should I wear and bring?
Closed-toe, sturdy walking shoes are required (no sandals, flip-flops, or Crocs); hiking boots or solid running shoes preferred. The badlands are hot and exposed with almost no shade, so bring a hat, sunscreen, and plenty of water, plus layers. Weather in June in Alberta can be variable, so check the forecast in advance. Bring insect repellent with DEET, and a camera or phone for photos.
Is there a café or place to buy food?
The Cretaceous Café operates seasonally (May to mid-October) with snacks and light meals. Outside that window, pack your own lunch. The Visitor Centre also has a gift shop for souvenirs.
How hot does it get?
Midsummer highs reach 25–30°C (sometimes hotter), and the near-total lack of shade makes it feel hotter still. Bring tons of water. The heat is part of the experience — the landscape looks more dramatic in harsh light — but it's real. Dress in layers and check the forecast.
What if the weather turns bad?
The park may cancel guided tours during extreme weather, including heavy rain or excessive heat. If conditions are questionable, check with Parkbus or the park before departure. In the event of rain, wet or unsafe road conditions, or anything that threatens participant safety and comfort, we may not be able to deliver the program as planned. We'll do our very best to offer an alternate program, but this can't be guaranteed.

The park itself: what you're seeing

Why is this a UNESCO World Heritage Site?
Three reasons: it holds the greatest concentration of Late Cretaceous dinosaur fossils on Earth (300+ complete skeletons removed since the 1880s, displayed in 30 cities); the badlands are a geological masterpiece carved by the Red Deer River over 13,000 years; and the ecosystem is unique — prairie grasslands at the rim, badlands in the valley, and cottonwood riparian forests along the river, three habitats in one protected area.
What dinosaurs lived here?
About 58 species have been discovered, from massive carnivores like Albertosaurus to herds of Centrosaurus (a horned dinosaur similar to Triceratops). Your guide will point out what you're seeing. The fossils date to about 75 million years ago, in the Late Cretaceous.
How do I know if I'm looking at a real fossil?
Your guide will point them out. There's an old trick: lick your finger and touch the rock — if it sticks slightly (because fossils are porous), it's a fossil; if it slides off, it's just rock. Kids love this test, and the guide will absolutely make you do it.
What's the Blackfoot connection?
The Blackfoot nation (Siksika) are the traditional territory holders and sacred homeland keepers of this land. They were the first to discover dinosaur bones and respectfully left them undisturbed. They called the hoodoos matapiiski, "the people," believing them spirits that watch over the land. Early camps were set on the grassland rim above the coulees, possibly out of spiritual respect. Your guide will share these stories.
What's a hoodoo?
A tall, thin rock formation created by selective erosion — softer sandstone erodes faster than the harder ironstone layers above it, creating spire-like shapes over millions of years. Sculptural and unsettling in the best way. You'll see dozens from the bus and the trails.
